About

My research develops trustworthy and efficient statistical methods for causal inference in modern data settings, with a particular focus on heterogeneous treatment effects. When treatment effects vary across individuals, natural questions arise on personalized treatments, adaptive trials, and identification of benefiting subgroups. These goals in turn raise challenges in selective inference and multiple testing due to the data-driven nature and multiplicity of subgroups.

I am also broadly interested in the interplay between machine learning and causal inference! Modern machine learning methods, such as neural networks and ML-assisted optimal transport, can help address causal problems with complex data; in turn, causal inference can contribute to model evaluation (which LLM shall I use?) and to explainability (which components of the ML moel are most influential?).

  • Research areas. Causal inference, heterogeneous treatment effects, randomization tests, adaptive clinical trials, partial identification, selective inference, and multiple testing.
  • Applications. Healthcare, experimentation platforms, and other data-driven decision settings.
  • Background. Ph.D. in Statistics from Stanford University; B.S. in Mathematics from Tsinghua University; Research Associate at the University of Cambridge before joining USC.
  • Teaching. BUAD 310: Applied Business Statistics (Spring 2024, Spring 2025, Spring 2026).

Research directions

These cards give visitors a fast overview of your work without making them read long publication abstracts on the homepage.

Causal inference with heterogeneity
Methods for estimating heterogeneous treatment effects, validating treatment effect estimators, and studying treatment interactions under realistic data settings.
Adaptive experimentation
Sequential and adaptive procedures for experimentation, randomization tests, and valid inference after data-dependent design or analysis choices.
Multiple testing and selective inference
Powerful procedures with rigorous error control, including recent work on adaptive Storey-type estimators and conformal novelty detection.
Optimal transport and partial identification
Using transport-based ideas to define interpretable causal bounds and tighten partial identification sets with covariate information.
